Who actually pays for ads in the terminal?

The advertiser bank, the money flow, pricing, targeting and geo — evidence-first, to feed the monetization GTM.

July 6, 2026 · Sources: live network sites & APIs (fetched today), two independent teardowns, HN/Product Hunt, funding press. Claims flagged verified single-source claimed

The verdict on the GTM assumption

Refuted

"There is an existing pool of advertisers already buying terminal inventory that we can win with lower CPM." The pool is real but tiny: ~4 brands plus a dozen agent-economy micro-startups, and the entire live WaitSpin market has cleared under €10 total. There is no spend to undercut.

Validated

"Real demand exists one level up." Gravity ($75M valuation) already fills a coding-agent network via API; Koah ($20.5M — Upwork, Skillshare) and ZeroClick ($55M — retail feeds) have funded brand demand and no dev-audience supply. Plugging in as their premium coding-agent supply is a live shortcut.

Reframe

The winnable advertiser is the Ramp/Sentry archetype — performance-run dev-tool and fintech buyers who already sponsor dev channels. They tried this in week one and left when nobody could prove the impressions. Measurement is the pitch, not price.

Are they after US users or global? Nobody in the terminal category is geo-buying in any meaningful way. Kickbacks buyers ship worldwide-by-default because volume is so small that slicing it makes campaigns undeliverable; WaitSpin can't slice at all. The audience skews US/EU by nature (Claude Code's installed base), so US-heavy advertisers like Ramp and the GLP-1 service get de-facto US delivery without asking for it. The strategic read: geo, tech-stack, and session-context targeting are whitespace — the "additional adtech value" you hypothesized genuinely does not exist here yet, except one layer up at Gravity.

Part 6

Why do they advertise here? Four motives, four business models

Motive 1 — Endemic distribution

Agent-economy startups

Who: Citedy/AdClaw/Lnkgo, here.now, ADSentry, Context.dev, Sponsoric's six.

Model: subscription/credit SaaS selling to agent operators. The person watching a Claude Code spinner is their exact ICP, so €5 CPM on a few hundred impressions is essentially free hyper-targeted distribution (~€2/campaign) plus novelty PR. These are the only buyers who persist — and the natural seed demand for any new network.

Motive 2 — Cheap experiment in a viral moment

Dev-channel performance buyers

Who: Ramp (fintech, interchange + SaaS), Sentry (PLG dev-tool SaaS), likely Feast.

Model: high-LTV products with permanent dev-media budgets (podcasts, TLDR, Reddit, Carbon/EthicalAds-class buys). They bought the launch spike as a brand test, got zero measurement back, and left. They advertise where developers are as policy — they'll return for whoever can prove the audience. This is the segment a real GTM wins.

Motive 3 — Self-dealing & optics

The networks themselves

Who: WaitSpin house ads (2 of 6 campaigns), Kickbacks' Firecrawl placeholder and "nasdaq_for_ads", earnd.dev buying on Kickbacks.

Model: filling empty inventory so the product doesn't look dead. Not demand — but it inflates every "advertisers" count you'll see quoted, so discount roster claims accordingly.

Motive 4 — Routed, not chosen

Aggregator spillover

Who: whatever Gravity's contextual pool holds (ADSentry observed); tomorrow, potentially Koah's Upwork/Skillshare class.

Model: the advertiser buys "AI contexts" from the aggregator and the terminal is just one placement in the mix — they may not know or care. This is the only demand that scales without a sales team, and it's how a new network gets day-one fill.

Part 8

Your original questions, answered straight

Who are the advertisers?

Verified real brands in the entire category, ever: Ramp, Sentry, Context.dev (YC S26), Feast — all on Kickbacks, all around the June 11 launch spike. verified Everything else observed serving is long tail: agent-economy micro-startups, a GLP-1 weight-loss service, a bible-research CLI, and — tellingly — competitor networks buying each other's inventory. No agencies, no Taboola/Outbrain-class arbitrage demand. These are the dev-channel sponsorship crowd plus founder friends.

What model do they pay on?

CPM, self-serve, everywhere. The category standard is "blocks" of 1,000 timed impressions (5s at Kickbacks, 10s at ClaudeCodeAds), bid via Stripe with a $0.50–$1 minimum, live in minutes, zero sales calls. Clicks exist as a CPM multiplier, not a CPC market — Kickbacks bills a click as 50 impressions. verified Exceptions: Rebates.ai pays a flat $0.005/impression; Sponsoric and PicoAds are CPC/affiliate paid in USDC. The adjacent chat-ads networks are the reverse: Koah is CPC-first (CPM only for awareness), ZeroClick is pure CPC.

If it's CPM — why is that good for them?

It isn't, and the market is saying so. CPM exists here because nobody can measure anything else: a one-line text ad in a terminal has no viewability standard, no pixel, and client-side counting that was demonstrably gameable (a documented Kickbacks bug let one bot earn ~$400/day). Advertisers responded by pricing it as junk: $8–42 CPM on marketing pages vs. an observed $1.09 clearing floor with no competitive bidding. verified

Do developers actually click and convert?

No evidence anywhere. Across every network: zero advertiser case studies, zero CTR disclosures, zero renewal signals. The strongest negative signal is behavioral: the launch-week buyers did not visibly return, and the market leader's queue reads "No queued bids — first bid takes #1 instantly" three weeks after a 5.5M-view launch. verified Contrast the chat-ads category, which publishes performance: Koah claims 3% CTR at scale (7.5% early) and $10 eCPM. claimed Intent lives where the user is asking a question — not where they're waiting for a diff.

Performance buyers, brands, or D2C?

Performance buyers wearing a brand-awareness costume. Ramp and Sentry are sophisticated dev-audience performance advertisers — Sentry states first-party that its highest-growth channels are technical YouTube, dev podcasts (it bought Syntax.fm), TLDR Web Dev and Reddit. Their terminal creatives are awareness one-liners because the channel can't attribute anything. The moment a network offers real attribution, these buyers can move budget on performance math — that's the wedge.

How were these advertisers brought in?

Not sold — harvested from a viral moment. Kickbacks' entire demand motion was Andrew McCalip's June 11 launch tweet (5.5M views) plus a $0.50-minimum self-serve form. Context.dev is a YC S26 company reaching the YC/dev crowd — network-proximity buying, not pipeline. No coding-agent network has a sales team, an agency relationship, or a pricing page aimed at a media buyer. Nobody in this category has ever actually sold this inventory. verified

Part 12

What this means for the GTM

  • Kill the "undercut on CPM" opener. At a $1 observed floor there is no price to beat and no incumbent spend to steal. Price is not the problem — proof is. Every documented failure is measurement-shaped: client-side counting, a $400/day bot, empty auction, throttled payouts.
  • Seed with the endemic segment (Motive 1). Agent-economy startups buy this inventory on their own initiative today at €5 CPM — an outbound list of Citedy-class agent-tool companies is a same-week fill strategy.
  • Sell to the archetype, not the channel's past buyers (Motive 2). The prospect database already exists: Carbon Ads / EthicalAds / BuySellAds buyer lists, TLDR & Console.dev sponsors, Changelog and Syntax.fm advertisers, YC dev-tool batches. First offer: measured pilot with server-side counting, fraud filtering, and cost-per-verified-session — CPM as a floor, not the product.
  • Plug into Gravity/Koah for day-one fill (Motive 4). The aicash→Gravity pipe proves an aggregator will monetize coding-agent supply today — solving cold-start while direct sales ramps.
  • Own the measurement narrative to educate the market. The category burned its first advertisers in three weeks. The share-of-voice play works, but the content is "here's why your terminal ads didn't work and how verified attention fixes it" — positioning MCG as the adult in a room of solo side-projects (13 of 14 networks are one-person, unincorporated, or dead).
  • Build the targeting nobody has. Geo, tech-stack, and session-context targeting are whitespace in the terminal category (Part 5). Idlen markets it and can't ship it; Gravity does it one layer up. Real targeting + real measurement is the "additional adtech value" wedge, exactly as hypothesized.
  • Watch the platform risk line. Anthropic's Super Bowl position ("Ads are coming to AI. But not to Claude") and the OpenCode legal precedent are the category's kill switch — keep the advertiser pitch multi-surface (CLI + IDE + chat bridge) rather than Claude-only.
